# Fundraising

Fundraising is a structured process of building investor conviction. It is not a sales process — it's a courtship that requires preparation, positioning, and timing.

## Valuation

### Common Valuation Methods

| Method | Description | Best For | Limitations |
|--------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| **Comparable Companies** | Multiple of revenue or EBITDA of public peers | Growth-stage SaaS | Few pure comparable public companies |
| **Comparable Transactions** | Multiple of revenue of recent acquisitions | M&A context, late-stage | Transaction data is often private |
| **DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)** | Present value of projected future cash flows | Profitable, predictable businesses | Highly sensitive to terminal value assumptions |
| **VC Method** | Estimate exit value, discount back | Early-stage venture | Highly dependent on exit assumptions |
| **Market Multiple** | Industry-standard multiples (5-10x ARR for SaaS) | Most SaaS companies | Oversimplified, ignores growth rate and efficiency |

### SaaS Valuation Reference (Public Market, 2024 Context)

| Metric | Low | Median | High |
|--------|-----|--------|------|
| EV / NTM Revenue | 3x | 6x | 12x+ |
| EV / NTM Gross Profit | 5x | 10x | 20x+ |
| Revenue growth rate premium | Growth >30% justifies >10x | Stable growth >15% at 5-8x | <15% growth at 3-5x |

### Factors That Increase Valuation

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Revenue growth rate (YoY) | High growth commands premium multiples |
| Gross margin (>75%) | Higher margins = higher multiples |
| Net Revenue Retention (>120%) | Expansion revenue signals product-market fit |
| Market size (TAM > $1B) | Large addressable market = larger potential outcome |
| Team quality | Repeat founders, domain expertise, prior exits |
| Competitive position | Category leader commands higher multiple |
| Capital efficiency | Low burn-to-revenue ratio signals discipline |

## Cap Table

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Authorized Shares** | Total shares the company is allowed to issue |
| **Issued Shares** | Shares that have been issued (founders, employees, investors) |
| **Fully Diluted Shares** | All issued shares + options + warrants + convertible notes (as-if converted) |
| **Option Pool** | Shares reserved for employee equity grants |
| **Outstanding Options** | Options that have been granted but not yet exercised |
| **Unallocated Pool** | Options not yet granted (available for future hires) |

### Cap Table Structure Example (Post-Seed)

| Holder | Shares | % Ownership | Type |
|--------|--------|-------------|------|
| Founders (2) | 8,000,000 | 64.0% | Common |
| Option Pool | 1,500,000 | 12.0% | Unallocated options |
| Seed Investor | 3,000,000 | 24.0% | Preferred |
| **Total** | **12,500,000** | **100.0%** | |

### Dilution Over Rounds (Typical)

| Round | Founders | Option Pool | Investors | Employees |
|-------|----------|-------------|-----------|-----------|
| Founding | 100% | 0% | 0% | 0% |
| Seed (20% to investors) | 80% | 10% (new) | 20% | 0% |
| Series A (25% to investors) | 56% | 14% | 40% | 4% (options exercised) |
| Series B (25% to investors) | 39.2% | 17% | 55% | 6% |
| Series C (20% to investors) | 31.4% | 18% | 64% | 7% |

Founder ownership naturally dilutes from 100% at founding to 15-30% at IPO. This is normal and expected.

### Cap Table Pitfalls

- **Not modeling dilution early.** Founders who don't model dilution are surprised when their ownership drops below 50% after a seed round.
- **Over-allocating the option pool.** Raising the pool too early dilutes founders without immediate benefit. Refresh the pool at each funding round.
- **Ignoring convertible note conversion.** Notes convert at the next round's valuation (often with a discount). Model the "as-if-converted" cap table before the round.
- **Complex structures (multiple classes, SAFEs, notes, warrants).** Simplicity wins. Multiple instruments create confusion in later rounds.

## Term Sheets

### Key Terms

**Economic Terms:**

| Term | Description | Typical Range |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| **Valuation** | Pre-money and post-money valuation | Market-dependent |
| **Investment Amount** | Cash invested | Per round |
| **Liquidation Preference** | How proceeds are distributed on exit | 1x (standard), sometimes 2x |
| **Participation** | Whether preferred shares participate in remaining proceeds after liquidation preference | Non-participating (standard), capped participation |
| **Anti-Dilution** | Protection if company raises at a lower valuation | Weighted average (standard), full ratchet (founder-unfriendly) |
| **Dividend Rights** | Preferred dividends, if any | Usually 8% cumulative or non-cumulative |

**Control Terms:**

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Board Composition** | Who sits on the board and how they're elected |
| **Protective Provisions** | Investor veto rights over specific actions (acquisition, change of BOD, debt, equity issuance) |
| **Drag-Along Rights** | Majority shareholders can force minority to participate in sale |
| **Information Rights** | Investor access to financial statements, metrics, and board materials |
| **Right of First Refusal (ROFR)** | Investor gets first chance to participate in future rounds |
| **Pro-Rata Rights** | Investor can maintain ownership percentage in future rounds |
| **No-Shop Clause** | Company cannot solicit other offers during the exclusivity period |

### Term Sheet Red Flags (Founder Perspective)

| Red Flag | Why |
|----------|-----|
| Full ratchet anti-dilution | Penalizes founders for down rounds far beyond what's fair |
| 2x+ participating preferred | Investor gets 2x their money back AND shares in remaining proceeds |
| Too many protective provisions | Investor can block normal operations |
| Excessive board control | Founders lose ability to make strategic decisions |
| Broad ROFR on employee shares | Investor can buy employee shares before they can sell them on secondary markets |

## The Fundraising Process

### Timeline

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Preparation** | 4-8 weeks | Data room, pitch deck, financial model, target list |
| **Outreach** | 2-4 weeks | Warm introductions to 30-50 investors |
| **Meetings** | 4-8 weeks | Initial meeting (30 min), deep dive (60 min), reference calls |
| **Due Diligence** | 4-6 weeks | Data room access, customer calls, reference checks, legal review |
| **Legal & Close** | 2-4 weeks | Term sheet negotiation, legal documents, closing conditions |

**Total timeline:** 4-6 months from start to money in the bank.

### Investor Targeting

| Investor Type | Check Size | Stage | Value Add |
|--------------|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Angel / Super-Angel | $25K-$500K | Pre-seed, Seed | Introductions, advice |
| Micro-VC | $500K-$3M | Seed, Series A | Domain expertise, network |
| Traditional VC | $3M-$15M | Series A, B | Brand, network, follow-on |
| Growth / Late Stage | $15M-$100M+ | Series C+ | Scale expertise, public market prep |
| Strategic / Corporate | Varies | Any | Distribution, partnership, M&A path |

### Data Room Contents

| Category | Documents |
|----------|-----------|
| **Corporate** | Certificate of incorporation, bylaws, cap table, board minutes |
| **Financial** | 3-year historical P&L, current financial model, unit economics, bank statements |
| **Product** | Product roadmap, architecture overview, technical debt assessment |
| **Market** | Competitive analysis, TAM/SAM/SOM, market trends, GTM plan |
| **Legal** | IP assignments, patents, contracts, employment agreements, privacy policy |
| **Customer** | Top 10 customer contracts, NPS data, case studies, churn analysis |
| **Team** | Org chart, key hire bios, recruiting plan, compensation benchmarking |

### Fundraising Pitfalls

- **Failing to create FOMO (fear of missing out).** Investors move slowly without competition. Create a process with a timeline, and give deadlines.
- **Over-optimistic forecasts.** A model that shows 300% YoY growth for 5 years destroys credibility. Show a realistic base case.
- **Running out of runway during the process.** Fundraising takes 4-6 months. Start when you have 9-12 months of runway remaining.
- **Talking to too many investors at once.** Investors talk to each other. A broad spray creates noise and damages your signal.
- **Ignoring the "no."** A polite "no" that doesn't close is not actionable. Push for a clear yes/no in every conversation.
- **Neglecting existing investors.** Your current investors are your best advocates. Keep them informed throughout the process.
