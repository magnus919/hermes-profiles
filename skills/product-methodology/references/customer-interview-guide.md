# Customer Interview Guide

Discovery interviews are the primary mechanism for identifying opportunities. They are not demos, not sales calls, not feature request sessions. They are structured explorations of how someone experiences a problem.

## Before the Interview

### Define the learning goal

What one thing do you want to understand better? A good learning goal is specific and falsifiable.

| Too vague | Specific |
|-----------|----------|
| "Learn how users manage their time" | "Understand how project managers decide which tasks to delegate vs which they keep" |
| "Find out if people want AI features" | "Identify the last time someone wished their project management tool could automate a decision for them" |

If you don't know what you want to learn, every interview will feel useful but none will teach you anything actionable.

### Recruit the right people

Talk to people who have the problem, not people who might have the problem someday. Signals:
- They've developed a workaround (spreadsheet, post-it notes, custom scripts)
- They've tried to solve the problem before (bought a tool, built a process)
- They can describe the last time it happened without prompting

Recruit 5-8 people per customer segment. You'll start hearing the same patterns around interview 4-5. That's the signal you're looking for — not more interviews, but deeper understanding of what you've heard.

### Write a discussion guide

Not a script — a guide. 5-7 broad questions. Leave room to follow interesting threads.

## The Interview Structure

### Opening (2 min)

Introduce yourself, set expectations:
- "I'm not selling anything — I'm trying to understand X better"
- "There are no wrong answers — I'm learning from you"
- "I'll take notes so I don't forget anything important"
- Ask permission to record (optional, but useful)

### The Story (25 min)

Ask about the LAST TIME they encountered the problem. Recent stories produce better data than general opinions.

**Good story questions:**
- "Tell me about the last time you had to [do X]. Walk me through what happened."
- "What was the hardest part about that?"
- "What did you try before you found this approach?"
- "What would have made that experience better?"

**Avoid:**
- "What features would you want?" (People are bad at designing solutions)
- "How much would you pay for this?" (People are bad at predicting their own behavior)
- "Would you use X if we built it?" (Polite people say yes)

### The Workaround (5 min)

Workarounds are the most honest signal of need. If someone built a spreadsheet, wrote a script, or created a process to solve a problem, that problem is real.

- "What did you do before you had this tool/process?"
- "What would you do if this tool didn't exist?"
- "Have you ever tried to build your own solution?"

### The Context (5 min)

Understand who they are without making it feel like a survey:
- Size of their team/company
- Their role and what their day looks like
- What tools they already use
- What they'd change about their current setup if they had a magic wand

### Closing (3 min)

- "What's the one thing you'd fix first if you could?"
- "Is there anything I should have asked but didn't?"
- "Would you be open to a follow-up if I have more questions?"

## After the Interview

### Write up within 24 hours

Capture:
- One-line summary of the most surprising thing you learned
- Key quotes (verbatim — these are gold for stakeholder buy-in)
- Opportunities identified (not solutions — needs, pains, desires)
- What you thought you'd learn vs what you actually learned

### Look for patterns across interviews

After 4-5 interviews, list every distinct opportunity you heard. Rank by:
- Frequency (how many people mentioned it)
- Intensity (how much pain did it cause)
- Consistency across segments (does everyone feel this, or just one type?)

### Update your Opportunity Solution Tree

Add new opportunities, prune ones that didn't show up in any interview, adjust confidence on existing nodes.

## Common Mistakes

**Talking too much.** You should speak about 20% of the time. If you're over 30%, you're leading the witness. A good interview feels like 80% listening and 20% clarifying.

**Asking hypotheticals.** "What would you do if..." produces fiction. "Tell me about the last time..." produces truth.

**Confirmation bias.** If you're only hearing what you expected, you're not listening hard enough. The most valuable interviews are the ones that surprise you.

**Status defense.** Senior people sometimes frame problems to sound like they have it under control. Ask about specific incidents ("last time this happened") rather than general patterns to get past the framing.

**The feature factory trap.** The output of a discovery interview should be opportunities, not feature requests. If an interview ends with a list of things to build, you didn't dig deep enough. Ask why each feature matters until you get to the underlying need.
