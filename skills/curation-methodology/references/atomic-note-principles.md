# Atomic Note Principles

Deciding what deserves its own note is the most important curation skill. Too broad and the note can't be recombined. Too narrow and the graph becomes noise.

## The Recombination Test

An atom is a single fact — one verifiable claim, one sentence can state it, and it's true without its source context. Before creating an atom, ask: *could this fact be useful in a molecule about a completely different topic?*

If yes → it's an atom.
If no → it's either source-anchored (belongs in the clipping) or it's too vague to be useful.

## What Belongs in an Atom

| Good candidates | Poor candidates |
|----------------|-----------------|
| A specific finding from a paper | A summary of a paper's entire conclusion |
| A named mechanism or principle | A general observation ("many researchers agree that...") |
| A quantitative claim with context | A number without source or methodology |
| A named person's stated position | Hearsay or unattributed opinion |
| A cause-effect relationship | Correlation presented as causation |
| A structural pattern | A one-off anecdote without broader significance |

## What Belongs in the Clipping, Not an Atom

- **The source's framing or narrative.** The clipping preserves the original context. The atom extracts the claim from it.
- **Procedural detail.** "The paper used a 5-layer CNN trained on ImageNet." That's methodology, not a claim. It belongs in the clipping unless the methodology itself is the finding.
- **The author's opinion without evidence.** "The author argues this is concerning" is not an atom. "The author shows that X causes Y under Z conditions" may be.
- **Negatives.** "Paper A did not find evidence for X" is not an atom. It's a research gap, which belongs in a molecule's analysis section.

## The Density Signal

If you're extracting from a source and producing fewer atoms than the vault-note skill's ratio tables suggest, you're either:
1. Summarizing instead of extracting (each atom rephrases a section instead of isolating a claim)
2. Discarding interesting claims because they don't seem directly relevant — keep them, relevance emerges through recombination
3. Grouping multiple claims into one atom — split them

Err toward more atoms. Merging is easy. Re-extracting from a source you no longer have open is expensive.

## When an Atom Is Too Broad

Signals:
- The atom's summary contains "and" — it's making multiple claims
- The atom references multiple mechanisms or findings
- The atom needs more than one sentence to state its claim
- The atom title is a compound sentence

**Fix:** Split into two atoms, each with a single claim. The molecule that needs both will reference both.

## When an Atom Is Too Narrow

Signals:
- The atom's claim is so specific it's unlikely to ever recombine
- The atom restates something already established in another atom
- The atom has zero incoming backlinks and it's been more than a month

**Options:**
1. Merge with a closely related atom if the claims are genuinely complementary
2. Let it live — some atoms are seeds that connect later
3. Delete only if it's a duplicate or demonstrably false
