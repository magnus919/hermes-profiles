# Stakeholder Communication

Different audiences need different information in different containers. At the C-suite level, communication is not just about clarity — it's about respecting the recipient's time, context, and decision authority.

## Executive Summaries

### The Format

```
RECOMMENDATION: [one sentence — the action you want]
WHY NOW: [the time sensitivity — 1-2 sentences]
KEY DATA: [the 3-5 facts that support the recommendation]
TRADE-OFFS: [what we give up or risk — 1-2 sentences]
NEXT STEPS: [who will do what by when]
```

### The Rules

**Lead with the recommendation.** Executives process top-down. Give them the answer first, then the supporting argument. If they need the methodology they'll ask.

**One page maximum.** If your executive summary is longer than one printed page, you haven't synthesized enough. The summary is not the full analysis — it's the gateway to it.

**Surface alternatives honestly.** Every recommendation should acknowledge the primary alternative and why it was not chosen. This pre-empts the most common executive question ("what about X?").

**Bad news in the first paragraph.** If the news is bad, surface it before the context. "We are going to miss Q3 revenue by 8%" is better than sharing three paragraphs of market context before the bad number.

**Attach the model, not the assumptions.** The executive summary should be self-contained. Supporting models, spreadsheets, and detailed analysis go in appendices. Never ask an executive to walk through your spreadsheet to find the conclusion.

### Anti-Patterns

- **The data dump.** "Here's 15 charts, figure it out yourself." The executive summary is your job, not theirs.
- **The humble brag.** "While we faced significant headwinds, the team heroically..." Executives just want the facts. Attribution is for skip-level meetings.
- **False urgency.** "We need a decision by end of day." If this isn't actually time-critical, you lose credibility for when it is.

## Board Decks

### The Format

A board deck is not a strategy document. It is a governance and oversight tool. The board's job is not to manage — it is to ensure the company is being managed well.

**Recommended structure (10-12 slides max):**

1. **Title Slide** — Quarter/key metric headline
2. **CEO Letter** — 1-page narrative. What happened, why it matters, what's ahead. Written in plain language, not bullet points.
3. **Financial Highlights** — Revenue, gross margin, operating expenses, cash. Variance vs plan. Trend (last 4 quarters).
4. **Operating Metrics** — ARR, NRR, churn (if SaaS). CAC, LTV, payback. Rule of 40. Lead with the 3-5 metrics that define health.
5. **Growth & Pipeline** — New logos, expansion, pipeline coverage. Key wins and losses.
6. **Product & Roadmap** — What shipped, what's coming, what slipped. Not a feature list — what capabilities are being built and why.
7. **People & Culture** — Headcount, attrition, key hires, succession, engagement. Board cares about talent risk.
8. **Risk & Governance** — Top 3-5 risks. Audit findings, compliance status. What we're doing about each.
9. **Ask & Guidance** — What we need from the board. Specific decisions, introductions, approvals.

### The Rules

**The board reads the appendix first.** The appendix (detailed financials, competitive analysis, full metrics) is where credibility is built. If the appendix is sloppy, the main deck is suspect.

**Variance is the story.** Don't show a chart of revenue without highlighting the variance from plan and explaining it. Flat vs plan is the same as growth vs plan — both need explanation.

**One message per slide.** A slide that tries to make three points makes zero. Each slide should have one clear takeaway.

**No surprises.** The board should never learn about a material issue from the slide deck. They should have heard it from you before the meeting. The deck is the documented version of what's already been communicated.

### Anti-Patterns

- **25+ slides.** You're covering too much. The board can read a detailed appendix. The meeting time is for discussion, not presentation.
- **"On track" for everything.** If everything is on track, either your targets are too easy or you're not being honest. The board sees through this immediately.
- **Deck-as-script.** If the deck can't stand alone as a document (because you plan to "talk through it"), send the full info in advance and use meeting time for Q&A.
- **Glossing over losses.** A lost deal that was in the pipeline is data. Present it along with wins. The board learns more from the losses than the wins.

## Strategy Memos

### The Format

A strategy memo is not a deck. It is a written document, typically 3-5 pages, that makes a specific strategic argument. It is the primary communication vehicle for strategic decisions.

**Recommended structure:**

1. **Executive Summary** (1 paragraph)
2. **Current Situation** — Where we are, how we got here, what's changing
3. **The Thesis** — What we propose to do and why it creates value
4. **Analysis** — Supporting data, reasoning, comparisons. This is the body of the argument.
5. **Alternatives Considered** — What else we looked at and why we chose this path
6. **Risks and Mitigations** — What could go wrong and what we'll do about it
7. **Resource Requirements** — What it costs (money, people, attention)
8. **Recommendation and Next Steps** — Specific asks with owners and dates

### The Rules

**Write in full paragraphs, not bullets.** A strategy memo should read like an argument. Bullet points are for reference documents. The memo is for persuasion.

**Assume the reader is smart but uninformed.** Don't explain basics, but do explain the context and reasoning they may not have. The board doesn't need to know how your product works — they need to know why this strategy makes sense for the market.

**Lead the counterargument.** Every strategic memo should include the strongest version of the opposing view within the document itself. If you don't address the counterarguments, the reader assumes you don't know them.

**The recommendation must be specific.** "Enter the European market" is too vague. "Acquire Company X for $50M to establish EU presence with a 18-month payback" is specific enough to evaluate.

### Common Failures

- **Describing vs arguing.** A memo that describes the market landscape without taking a position is a report, not a strategy memo.
- **Buried recommendation.** If the recommendation comes on page 4 of 5, the reader spent 80% of their time on setup before getting to the point.
- **Analysis without synthesis.** 15 data points without a conclusion is homework, not a strategy memo. The value is in the interpretation.

## Crisis Communication

### The Format

When something goes wrong, the first communication follows a specific pattern:

1. **Acknowledge the situation** — "We are aware of [issue] affecting [scope]."
2. **State what you know and don't know** — Be precise about the boundary.
3. **State what you're doing** — Specific actions, not "we're looking into it."
4. **State when you'll communicate next** — A specific time, not "soon."
5. **Provide a point of contact** — One person, with their direct contact info.

### The Rules

**Speed over perfection.** The first communication doesn't need to have all answers. It needs to exist and demonstrate that you are aware and acting. A 15-minute "we know and we're working on it" is better than a 2-hour "here's everything" that took 2 hours to write.

**One voice.** All external communication comes from one designated person. Internal communication through the chain of command. No one else speculates publicly.

**Never lie, never guess.** If you don't know the root cause, don't speculate. "We're investigating and will share what we find" is the only acceptable answer until you know.

**The bad news does not get better with time.** If you know something is bad but haven't figured out the full scope, share what you know now. Holding bad news to "get more information" destroys trust faster than the bad news itself.
