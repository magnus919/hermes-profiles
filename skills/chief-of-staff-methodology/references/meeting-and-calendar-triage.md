# Meeting & Calendar Triage

The leader's calendar is the most visible expression of their priorities — and the
most common source of their overwhelm. The Chief of Staff manages the calendar not
as a scheduling exercise, but as a **strategic allocation of the leader's scarcest
resource: attention.**

## The Calendar Audit

Before optimizing, measure. A quarterly calendar audit reveals where the leader's
time actually goes vs. where it should go.

### Audit Categories

| Category | Definition | Target % |
|----------|------------|----------|
| **Strategic** | Work only the leader can do — vision, strategy, key relationships, major decisions | 30-40% |
| **Tactical** | Work the leader should do but could be delegated with better systems — team reviews, status updates | 20-30% |
| **Operational** | Work someone else should do — recurring meetings, status reports, approvals | 10-20% |
| **Consumption** | Work that consumes without producing — all-hands, industry events, unscheduled interruptions | <10% |
| **Buffer** | Thinking time, strategic reading, relationship building, recovery | 15-20% |

**Warning sign:** If "Operational" exceeds 30% or "Buffer" is below 10%, the leader
is being consumed by their calendar rather than leading through it.

## Meeting Triage Framework

Every meeting that includes the leader should justify its existence against this
framework:

### Does This Meeting Need the Leader?

| Meeting Type | Leader Needed? | Alternative |
|-------------|----------------|-------------|
| Decision that only the leader can make | Yes | — |
| Decision within someone else's authority | No | Route to the decision-maker |
| Status update with no decision needed | No | Async update (doc, email, Slack) |
| Cross-functional coordination | No | CoS attends as proxy |
| Relationship building with key partner | Yes (some) | CoS preps, leader attends key sessions |
| Team all-hands / cultural event | Strategic | CoS can represent if leader's time is scarce |
| Industry event / conference | Selective | Rotate attendance across the team |
| 1:1 with direct report | Yes (some) | Audit frequency — weekly may be too much; monthly may be enough |

### The Meeting Cost Equation

```
Meeting cost = (# participants × meeting minutes × blended hourly rate)
```

Communicate this cost to the leader. A 60-minute meeting with 8 people at a $200/hr
blended rate costs $1,600. Is that the best use of $1,600?

### Meeting Classification Tags

Tag every recurring meeting with the leader:

| Tag | Meaning | Action |
|-----|---------|--------|
| **Retain** | High value, needs the leader | Protect |
| **Delegate** | Valuable but someone else can attend | Send a proxy, brief them, debrief later |
| **Async** | No live interaction needed | Replace with doc + async comments |
| **Reduce** | Valuable but frequency is too high | Drop cadence from weekly to bi-weekly or monthly |
| **Eliminate** | No longer serves its purpose | Cancel |

## Calendar Design Principles

### Protect Strategic Time

The leader gets **uninterrupted strategic blocks** — 2-4 hours, no meetings, no
interruptions. These are non-negotiable and scheduled first. Everything else fills
in around them.

- **Frequency:** At least 2 per week
- **Length:** 2-4 hours minimum
- **Protection:** No exceptions unless there is a genuine crisis
- **Placement:** Match the leader's energy pattern (morning deep work for most leaders)

### Batch Low-Value Work

Operational and consumption activities get batched into specific time blocks:

- All approvals at 4pm daily (not scattered through the day)
- All interviews on Tuesday/Thursday mornings
- All external meetings on Wednesdays
- All internal status meetings at the same time

### Build Transition Time

Every meeting needs a buffer before and after. A 60-minute meeting is really 75
minutes (15 min to prepare, 60 min to attend, 15 min to decompress and capture
action items). Schedule accordingly.

### Strategic Calendar Preview

The CoS produces a rolling 4-week calendar preview:

- **Week 1:** Finalized — decisions, prep, materials
- **Week 2:** Locking — major events confirmed, gaps identified
- **Week 3-4:** Shaping — strategic priorities, key decisions coming

## Meeting Preparation Standard

Before every meeting the leader attends, the CoS provides:

1. **Why this meeting** — one-sentence purpose and what success looks like
2. **What's expected of the leader** — decide, advise, approve, or just listen?
3. **Key context** — what happened last time, what's changed, what's at stake
4. **Participants** — who's in the room, what each person wants from the leader
5. **Pre-reading** — anything the leader should have read before attending (with page numbers highlighted)
6. **Suggested talking points** — 2-3 points the leader should make if relevant
7. **Landmines** — sensitive topics, unresolved tensions, people who might derail the conversation

## Calendar Triage Heuristics

| Rule | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| No meeting without an agenda | If it's not worth preparing for, it's not worth attending |
| Default to 30 minutes | Most 60-minute meetings can be 30. Most 30-minute meetings can be 15. |
| Standing meetings default to bi-weekly | Weekly is usually a habit, not a requirement |
| No internal meetings before 10am or after 4pm | Protect deep work time on both ends |
| The leader never books their own calendar | The CoS owns the calendar. The leader is the only client. |
| Every recurring meeting reviewed quarterly | If it can't justify its existence, it's cancelled |
| Decline by default for unscheduled requests | "Let me check" buys the CoS time to triage properly |

## The Weekly Calendar Review

Every Friday, the CoS reviews the next week's calendar with the leader:

1. **Are the strategic blocks protected?** — No unexpected meetings have crept in
2. **Is the load sustainable?** — Back-to-back days need buffers
3. **Are the right meetings prioritized?** — Is the leader spending time where they add the most value?
4. **What meetings can be cut or delegated?** — Review the meeting classification tags
5. **What needs preparation?** — Assign pre-reading, briefs, and materials

## Common Calendar Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | Signal | Fix |
|-------------|--------|-----|
| **Strategic starvation** | Calendar is full but nothing strategic got done | Protect strategic blocks ruthlessly. Move everything else. |
| **Meeting sprawl** | New recurring meetings appear without review | Every recurring meeting needs a sunset date. Quarterly review. |
| **Decision bottlenecks** | Key decisions are delayed because the leader is in meetings | Schedule decision blocks. Pre-wire decisions as memos. |
| **Context switching tax** | The leader has 10+ meetings a day with no buffers | Batch similar meetings. Add 15-min buffers between meetings. |
| **Proxy confusion** | Meetings where the CoS or a delegate attended but no handoff happened | Brief before, debrief after. Never attend without a clear mandate. |
