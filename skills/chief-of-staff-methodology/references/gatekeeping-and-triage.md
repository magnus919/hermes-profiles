# Gatekeeping & Triage

The most visible — and most misunderstood — function of the Chief of Staff. Gatekeeping
is not about building walls. It is about designing an information filter that ensures
the leader sees the right things at the right time and in the right format.

## Core Principle: Decisions Per Unit Time

The leader's scarcest resource is not money, talent, or strategy — it is **attention**.
Every piece of information that reaches them consumes attention. Every meeting they
attend consumes attention. Every decision they make consumes attention.

The CoS triages against one question: **"Does this need the leader specifically, or
does it just need a decision?"**

## The Triage Stack

### Tier 0: Never reaches the leader
- Information the leader already knows or doesn't need to act on
- Decisions that fall within someone else's authority
- Issues that can be resolved with existing precedent or policy
- Noise — inbound requests from people who want the leader's attention but don't need it

The CoS resolves these themselves or routes them to the right person. The leader never
hears about them.

### Tier 1: Reaches the leader as a brief
- Information the leader should be aware of but doesn't need to decide on
- Updates on previously delegated decisions
- Trends, patterns, or early signals worth watching
- Positive outcomes of work the leader cares about

Format: A paragraph or bullet points, no more than 3-5 items per brief. Delivered
periodically (daily standup, weekly brief), not interrupt-driven.

### Tier 2: Reaches the leader as a decision memo
- Decisions only the leader can make (by authority, risk, or strategy)
- Decisions with significant trade-offs that affect multiple domains
- Requests for resources, headcount, or budget changes
- Escalations from Tier 1 that became urgent

Format: Structured memo — one-page max, problem statement, options, recommendation,
required resources, risks. The CoS has already vetted and prepared the context.

### Tier 3: Reaches the leader as an interrupt
- Crises that need immediate leader attention
- Decisions that cannot wait for the next briefing cycle
- Information that changes a strategic assumption

Format: Verbal or brief written alert. The CoS has verified the facts before
interrupting. The leader is told what happened, what has already been done, and
what decision is needed right now.

## The Gatekeeper's Checklist

Before any item reaches the leader, ask:

1. **Does this need the leader's decision, or just a decision?** If the latter, route it to the person who has authority.
2. **Has this been vetted?** Have you checked the facts, consulted the relevant people, prepared the alternatives?
3. **What format does this deserve?** A brief, a decision memo, or an interrupt? Match the format to the urgency and complexity.
4. **Is this the right time?** Is the leader in a context where they can receive this? (Not during a stressful meeting, not right before a board presentation, not at 5pm on Friday.)
5. **What can I do first?** Can you narrow the options to 2-3? Can you get input from the relevant stakeholders? Can you draft the delegation message?

## The "No That Sticks"

The hardest gatekeeping is saying no in a way that preserves relationships. The CoS
doesn't just block — they redirect.

- **"The CEO doesn't need to see this. Let me connect you with [person] who owns this — they can make the decision directly."**
- **"This isn't the right time. Here's what needs to happen first before this reaches the CEO."**
- **"The CEO doesn't make vendor decisions — that's [VP]'s call. I'll make sure the CEO knows you reached out."**

## Escalation Paths

Document and maintain explicit escalation rules:

| Type | Escalates To | When |
|------|-------------|------|
| Operational | Relevant VP or department head | Day-to-day issues within a domain |
| Cross-functional | CoS coordinates with relevant leads | Issues spanning multiple domains |
| Strategic | Leader + relevant exec | Decisions with long-term implications |
| Crisis | Leader immediately | Safety, reputation, financial, legal risk |

The escalation path should be known to everyone who reports to the leader. The CoS
enforces it. Anyone who bypasses it without justification gets redirected back.

## Common Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | Signal | Fix |
|-------------|--------|-----|
| **Bottleneck** | Everything stalls at the CoS — nothing reaches the leader, but nothing gets decided either | Audit your triage stack. Are you resolving enough at Tier 0? Are you delegating decision authority? |
| **Filter failure** | Leader keeps receiving things they shouldn't have to see | Tighten Tier 0. Ask "why did this reach me?" after every unnecessary interrupt |
| **Organizational friction** | Teams feel blocked from reaching the leader | The gate should make the *right* things flow faster, not everything flow slower |
| **CoS as single point of failure** | When the CoS is out, nothing happens | Document triage rules, train deputies, build systems that work without you |
| **Over-delegation to Tier 2** | The leader only sees memos, never gets context or feels disconnected | Maintain a regular cadence of Tier 1 briefs so the leader stays informed even about things they didn't ask about |
