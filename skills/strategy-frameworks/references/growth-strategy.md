# Growth Strategy

Frameworks for evaluating growth opportunities, entering new markets, and balancing short-term and long-term investments.

## Ansoff Matrix

A classic framework for categorizing growth strategies based on whether you're selling existing/new products into existing/new markets.

### The Matrix

| | Existing Markets | New Markets |
|---|-----------------|-------------|
| **Existing Products** | **Market Penetration** | **Market Development** |
| **New Products** | **Product Development** | **Diversification** |

### Strategy Details

#### Market Penetration (Existing products, existing markets)

Lowest risk. Grow share in your current market through pricing, promotion, distribution, or acquisition.

**When to use:** Market is not saturated, you have a competitive advantage, or competitors are weak.

**Tactics:**
- Price reductions or promotions
- Increased marketing spend
- Channel expansion (new partner types)
- Acquire a competitor's customer base
- Improve retention and reduce churn

**Risk:** Very low. But the market may be finite, and penetration beyond a certain point becomes expensive.

#### Product Development (New products, existing markets)

Medium risk. Leverage your existing customer relationships and market knowledge to sell new things.

**When to use:** You have strong customer relationships and distribution, your customers have unmet needs, or you're at risk of being replaced.

**Tactics:**
- New features for existing products
- Adjacent product lines
- Platform extensions
- Bundling or unbundling existing offerings

**Risk:** Medium. You know the customers but may not know whether they want the new product.

#### Market Development (Existing products, new markets)

Medium risk. Take what you have and sell it to new customer segments or geographies.

**When to use:** Your current market is saturated, you have a product that solves a universal problem, or you found a new segment with the same need.

**Tactics:**
- Geographic expansion (new city, country, region)
- New customer segments (enterprise → SMB, or vice versa)
- New channels (direct → partner, online → retail)
- New use cases (position existing product for different job)

**Risk:** Medium-High. Different markets have different regulations, culture, competition, and distribution.

#### Diversification (New products, new markets)

Highest risk. New products for new customers. You know neither.

**When to use:** Current market is dying, you have a unique capability that applies elsewhere, or you're hedging existential risk.

**Tactics:**
- Acquire a company in a different market
- Build a new business unit from scratch
- Joint venture with a company that has market access

**Risk:** Very high. Most diversification fails. The risk can be reduced if there's a shared capability (e.g., the same technology applied to a different market).

### Ansoff Decision Matrix

| Strategy | Risk | Required Capability | Time to Results |
|----------|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Market Penetration | Low | Marketing, sales execution | 3-6 months |
| Product Development | Medium | R&D, customer insight | 6-18 months |
| Market Development | Medium-High | Market knowledge, localization | 12-24 months |
| Diversification | High | New capability building | 24-48 months |

## Three Horizons of Growth

A framework for balancing investment across three time horizons. The core insight: companies that only invest in the near term will die, but companies that only invest in the far term will starve.

### The Horizons

| Horizon | Timeframe | Focus | Funding | Metrics |
|---------|-----------|-------|---------|---------|
| **H1: Core Business** | Now - 18 months | Defend and extend the core | Self-funding | Revenue, profit, share |
| **H2: Adjacent Growth** | 18-36 months | Build new growth engines | Partially funded by H1 | User growth, pipeline, early revenue |
| **H3: Emerging Options** | 3-7 years | Seed long-term bets | Funded by strategic budget | Learning, IP, team, options created |

### Allocation Guidelines

| Company Stage | H1 | H2 | H3 |
|--------------|-----|-----|-----|
| Startup (<$10M ARR) | 70% | 25% | 5% |
| Growth ($10-100M ARR) | 60% | 30% | 10% |
| Scale ($100M+ ARR) | 50% | 30% | 20% |
| Mature | 80% | 15% | 5% |

These are starting points. Adjust based on market dynamics, competitive pressure, and growth trajectory.

### Horizon Governance

**H1: Managed through OKRs and the annual planning process.**
- Focus: efficiency, incremental improvement, share capture
- Review: Monthly operating reviews

**H2: Managed as separate P&L or business unit.**
- Focus: validated learning, product-market fit, repeatable sales motion
- Review: Bi-weekly standups, quarterly strategic reviews
- Protection: Shield from H1 short-term pressures

**H3: Managed through a separate innovation fund or incubator.**
- Focus: experimentation, option creation, learning velocity
- Review: Stage-gate process, kill criteria
- Protection: Immune to quarterly earnings pressure

### Three Horizons Pitfalls

- **H2 neglect.** Most companies understand H1 (run the business) and H3 (R&D), but H2 — the bridge — is where execution gaps kill growth. H2 needs dedicated leadership and separate metrics.
- **Horizon creep.** H3 initiatives that don't graduate to H2 or get killed accumulate and drain resources. Establish clear gate criteria and enforce them.
- **Allocating by politics, not strategy.** H3 funding should be based on strategic logic, not on which executive advocates loudest. Use a transparent allocation process.
- **Treating all H3 bets the same.** A portfolio of H3 bets should span different timeframes, risk profiles, and market opportunities.

## Market Entry Strategy

Entering a new market is one of the highest-risk strategic moves. Success depends on choosing the right mode of entry and having a clear thesis about why you will win.

### Entry Mode Options

| Mode | Commitment | Risk | Control | Speed | When to Choose |
|------|------------|------|--------|-------|----------------|
| **Organic** | Build from scratch | Lowest | Full | Slowest | Core capability overlap, time to learn |
| **Acquisition** | Buy existing player | Highest | Full | Fastest | Need instant market presence, willing to pay |
| **Partnership** | Align with local player | Moderate | Shared | Medium | Speeds market access, reduces risk |
| **Joint Venture** | Create new entity | High | Shared | Medium | Regulatory requirement, risk sharing |
| **Licensing** | License technology/rights | Low | Low | Fast | Low commitment, test the market |

### Market Entry Checklist

Before entering any new market, have a clear answer to each:

| Question | Good Answer | Bad Answer |
|----------|-------------|------------|
| Why will customers buy from us? | We solve a validated pain better than incumbents | It's a big market |
| Why now? | Market timing factor (regulation, technology shift) | Seems like a good time |
| Who is the incumbents? | Named competitors with known strengths | Generic "fragmented market" |
| How will they respond? | Specific retaliation scenarios | They won't notice us |
| What's the beachhead? | One specific segment/geography | "We'll target everyone" |
| How long until breakeven? | 12-24 months with specific assumptions | "Fast" |
| What's the exit if it fails? | Known sunk cost and recovery path | "We won't fail" |

### Market Entry Pitfalls

- **The "big market" trap.** A big market does not make a good entry strategy. Many companies fail in large markets because they can't gain traction against incumbents.
- **Underestimating incumbents' response.** Incumbents have relationships, data, and economic moats you don't see from the outside. Assume they will respond aggressively.
- **Half-hearted commitment.** The worst entry strategy is organic entry with insufficient resources and no clear path to leadership. Either commit fully or partner.
- **Cultural blind spots.** Entering a new geography without local leadership, cultural competence, or regulatory understanding is nearly guaranteed to fail.
