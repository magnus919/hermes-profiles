# Resource Allocation

Frameworks for deploying capital, evaluating M&A opportunities, and managing a portfolio of investments.

## Capital Allocation Strategy

Capital allocation is the CEO's most important recurring decision. How the company deploys its capital determines its trajectory more than any individual product or hiring decision.

### Capital Deployments

| Use | Return Profile | When to Prioritize |
|-----|---------------|-------------------|
| **Reinvest in the business** | Depends on unit economics | Current ROIC > WACC and growth runway remains |
| **Acquisitions** | Highly variable | Strategic fit, reasonable price, integration capacity |
| **Return to shareholders** | Buyback: accretive if undervalued | No high-ROI internal projects, excess capital |
| **Pay down debt** | Saves interest expense | High leverage, rising rates, covenant concerns |
| **Build reserves** | Opportunity cost of idle cash | High uncertainty, potential strategic opportunity |

### Allocation Framework

1. **Calculate ROIC (Return on Invested Capital).** Is the core business generating returns above the cost of capital? If not, the priority is fixing the core — not allocating to growth.
2. **Identify reinvestment capacity.** How much capital can the core business productively absorb at current ROIC levels? Beyond this point, marginal returns decline.
3. **Set the allocation budget.** After core reinvestment and debt service, how much is available for strategic bets?
4. **Apply hurdle rates.** Every dollar of capital must have a clear expected return threshold. Higher-risk allocations require higher return expectations.
5. **Review and rotate.** Rebalance allocation based on performance and changing market conditions.

### Capital Allocation Pitfalls

- **Reinvesting because "that's what we do."** If the core business has declining ROIC, pouring more capital into it destroys value. Consider returning capital instead.
- **The endowment effect on business units.** Division leaders argue for more capital based on past allocation, not future returns. Require every capital request to present expected returns.
- **Hoarding cash.** Holding excessive cash "for a rainy day" that never comes is a drag on returns. Have a clear capital return policy for excess cash.
- **Ignoring opportunity cost.** Every dollar allocated to one use is a dollar not allocated to another. Make the trade-off explicit in every capital decision.

## M&A Evaluation

Acquisitions are the highest-risk form of capital allocation. The majority destroy value for the acquirer. A rigorous evaluation framework reduces the odds of failure.

### Strategic Fit Assessment

| Fit Dimension | Questions |
|--------------|-----------|
| **Market** | Does this expand our TAM, enter an adjacent market, or consolidate our position? |
| **Product/Capability** | Does this fill a gap in our product, technology, or IP? |
| **Talent** | Does this bring critical skills or leadership we can't hire? |
| **Distribution** | Does this give us access to customers or channels we don't have? |
| **Competitive** | Does this neutralize a threat or create a competitive advantage? |

### Valuation Framework

| Method | Use Case | Approach |
|--------|----------|----------|
| **DCF** | Cash-flow-generating businesses | Project FCF, discount at WACC |
| **Comparable transactions** | Any — establishes market range | Recent acquisitions of similar companies |
| **Trading comps** | Public company targets | Revenue/EBITDA multiples of public peers |
| **LBO model** | PE-style buyout | Returns to equity holder given debt structure |
| **Synergy value** | Always required | Quantified cost savings + revenue synergies |

### Synergy Analysis

Most acquired synergies are over-estimated and under-delivered. Be rigorous:

**Cost Synergies (more predictable, 60-80% achievement):**
- Headcount reduction in overlapping functions
- Facility consolidation
- Vendor consolidation (volume discounts)
- Technology stack consolidation

**Revenue Synergies (less predictable, 30-50% achievement):**
- Cross-sell to combined customer base
- Geographic expansion via combined distribution
- Bundled product offerings
- Accelerated roadmap via combined R&D

### Integration Planning

The integration plan must be developed BEFORE the deal closes, not after.

| Phase | Timeline | Activities |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Day 1 Readiness** | Pre-close | Leadership alignment, Day 1 communications, legal structure |
| **First 100 Days** | Close + 100 days | Org structure, retention, system migration plan, customer communications |
| **First Year** | 3-12 months | Technical integration, process standardization, cultural integration |
| **Value Realization** | 12-36 months | Synergy tracking, performance monitoring, course correction |

### M&A Anti-Patterns

- **CEO attachment.** The CEO falls in love with the target and stops seeing the risks. Separate the diligence team from the negotiating team.
- **Paying for future, not for present.** Valuing a startup on their projected growth without accounting for the risk that they miss those projections.
- **Ignoring cultural fit.** The hardest part of integration is culture clash. Assess culture with the same rigor as financials.
- **Integration resource starvation.** The best deal fails if the integration team is pulled in two directions. Dedicate full-time integration leaders.
- **Underestimating retention.** Key people at the target will leave without retention packages, clear roles, and cultural integration.

## Portfolio Management

When a company has multiple business units, products, or investments, portfolio management frameworks help balance risk, return, and strategic fit.

### BCG Growth-Share Matrix

| | High Market Growth | Low Market Growth |
|---|-------------------|-------------------|
| **High Relative Share** | **Stars** — Invest for growth | **Cash Cows** — Harvest cash |
| **Low Relative Share** | **Question Marks** — Decide: invest or divest | **Dogs** — Divest |

**Strategy by Quadrant:**

| Quadrant | Strategic Approach | Capital Allocation |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Star | Invest aggressively to maintain leadership | Significant investment |
| Cash Cow | Maximize cash generation, minimize investment | Minimal maintenance |
| Question Mark | Selective investment or divestiture | Targeted bets, clear kill criteria |
| Dog | Divest, harvest, or reposition | Minimal to none |

### Portfolio Assessment Questions

For each business unit or investment:

1. **Strategic fit.** Is this core to our mission? Does it give us an advantage we can't get elsewhere?
2. **Competitive position.** Are we winning or losing? Is the trend improving or declining?
3. **Capital efficiency.** Does this unit generate returns above our cost of capital?
4. **Growth trajectory.** Is the unit in a growing market? Is it gaining or losing share?
5. **Management quality.** Do we have the right leadership team? If not, can we fix it?
6. **Exit value.** If we sold this unit today, what would it be worth?

### Portfolio Pitfalls

- **Holding losers too long.** Sunk cost fallacy. If a business unit isn't meeting hurdle rates and has no credible turnaround plan, divest.
- **Starving stars.** Cash cows generate cash that gets spread across the portfolio instead of reinvested in the stars that will become the next cash cows.
- **Over-diversification.** Adding businesses that don't share capabilities or customers. Synergy requires overlap — a portfolio of unrelated businesses is a holding company, not a strategy.
- **Annual portfolio review is not enough.** In fast-moving markets, portfolio assumptions can change in a quarter. Review flagship assumptions more frequently.
