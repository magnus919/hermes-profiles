# Strategic Planning

Frameworks for setting organizational direction, defining what matters, and communicating strategy to all stakeholders.

## OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

OKRs connect organizational ambition to measurable outcomes. The framework is simple; the discipline is not.

### Structure

- **Objective:** What you want to accomplish. Aspirational, qualitative, time-bound.
- **Key Results:** 3-5 measurable outcomes that define success for the objective. Quantitative and verifiable.

### Writing Good OKRs

**Good Objective:**
> Establish market leadership in the mid-market segment

**Bad Objective:**
> Improve sales (too vague, not inspirational)

**Good Key Results:**
> - KR1: Grow ARR from $5M to $8M in mid-market
> - KR2: Achieve NRR of 120%+ in the segment
> - KR3: Close 3 referenceable logos with >$500K ACV

**Bad Key Results:**
> - "Launch new features" (not measurable)
> - "Improve sales process" (not an outcome)
> - "Work harder" (not verifiable)

### OKR Best Practices

| Practice | Why |
|----------|-----|
| 3-5 KRs per objective | More than 5 means you haven't prioritized. Fewer than 3 means the objective is too narrow. |
| KRs are outcomes, not tasks | "Ship feature X" is a task. "40% of users activate feature X within 7 days" is an outcome. |
| 60-80% completion is ideal | 100% on every KR suggests the bar was too low. A stretch goal should feel uncomfortable. |
| Company OKRs → Team OKRs → Individual OKRs | Alignment, not cascade. Teams should derive from company OKRs, not just receive them as top-down assignments. |
| Grades are diagnostic, not punitive | A KR that misses at 40% tells you something about your planning. The score is data, not a performance review. |

### Common OKR Anti-Patterns

- **Committed + aspirational confusion.** Some teams use two tiers of OKRs. Pick one and be clear about the expectation. Either it's a commitment (100% or explain) or a stretch (70% is fine).
- **OKRs as a task list.** If your KR is "Launch the mobile app," that's a milestone, not a key result. The KR should be about adoption, not shipping.
- **The "everyone gets 100%" culture.** If all OKRs score 100%, the company is sandbagging. The planning process needs intervention.
- **Too many OKRs.** One company OKR per quarter. Period. If a company has 5 OKRs, it has none — everything is equally important, which means nothing is.

## Mission, Vision, Values

### Definitions

| Term | Question It Answers | Time Horizon | Characteristics |
|------|--------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Mission** | Why do we exist? | Present tense | A statement of purpose, not goals. Describes what the organization does daily. |
| **Vision** | Where are we going? | Future (3-10 years) | An aspirational picture of the future. Should feel ambitious but plausible. |
| **Values** | How do we behave? | Permanent | Decision-making principles that guide behavior when no one is watching. |

### Writing a Mission Statement

A good mission statement is:

1. **Specific to your organization.** Could it apply to your biggest competitor? If yes, rewrite it.
2. **Action-oriented.** "To organize the world's information" (Google) describes an ongoing activity, not a destination.
3. **Enduring.** It should still be relevant in 10+ years.
4. **Memorable.** If your team can't recite it, it's too long.

**Examples:**
- "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy" (Tesla)
- "To give people the power to share and make the world more open and connected" (Facebook, original)
- "To organize the world's information and make it universally accessible and useful" (Google)

### Writing a Vision Statement

A vision describes what success looks like at a specific future date. It should be vivid enough that someone outside the company would understand what you will have accomplished.

**Template:**
> By [year], we will have [accomplished X]. This means [what will be different] for [stakeholder group]. We will know we've succeeded when [verifiable evidence].

### Values That Work

- **3-5 values maximum.** More than 5, and none of them stick.
- **Behaviorally specific.** "Integrity" is too vague. "We say the hard thing in the room" is specific enough to evaluate.
- **Trade-off revealing.** The best values are the ones that tell you what NOT to do. "Customer obsession" means you don't build things because they're technically interesting.
- **Lived, not laminated.** If values are only discussed during onboarding, they aren't values — they're wallpaper.

## Strategic Narrative

A strategic narrative is the story of why the organization exists, where it's going, and how it will get there. It is the single thread that connects mission to quarterly OKRs.

### The Narrative Structure

1. **The World (Context)** — What is changing in the market, technology, or customer behavior that creates an opportunity or threat?
2. **The Insight (Why Us)** — What do we understand or have that others don't? Our unique insight, capability, or position.
3. **The Ambition (Where)** — Where we're going. Specific, compelling, and time-bound.
4. **The Path (How)** — The strategic approach, not the operational plan. The approach to competing and winning.
5. **The Risk (Why It Might Fail)** — Honest acknowledgment of what could derail us. Builds trust and focuses attention on the real threats.

### Using the Narrative

- **Investor communication:** The narrative replaces the old "problem → solution → market size" pitch format.
- **All-hands:** Use the narrative to connect individual work to company direction. Every team should be able to explain how their OKRs connect to the narrative.
- **Hiring:** The narrative sells better than a comp package.
- **Strategic decisions:** Test every major decision against the narrative. "Does this advance the story we're telling?"

### Narrative vs Strategy Document

| Narrative | Strategy Document |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Story, not analysis | Analysis, not story |
| Answered in 3-5 minutes | Takes 30+ minutes to read |
| Designed to be told and retold | Designed to be referenced and updated |
| Inspires action | Justifies action |
| Lives in all-hands, pitches, onboarding | Lives in board decks, planning docs |
