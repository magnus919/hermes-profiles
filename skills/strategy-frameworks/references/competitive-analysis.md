# Competitive Analysis

Frameworks for understanding competitive position, assessing moat durability, and identifying strategic opportunities in the market landscape.

## Porter's Five Forces

A framework for analyzing the competitive structure of an industry. The five forces determine the long-run profitability of an industry.

### The Five Forces

| Force | Definition | High Threat Means | Low Threat Means |
|-------|------------|-------------------|------------------|
| **Rivalry Among Existing Competitors** | Intensity of competition among current players | Many competitors, slow growth, high fixed costs, low differentiation | Few competitors, fast growth, high switching costs |
| **Threat of New Entrants** | How easy is it for new companies to enter? | Low barriers (no regulation, no brand, low capital) | High barriers (patents, network effects, economies of scale) |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | Can customers solve the same problem in a different way? | Many alternatives, low switching cost to substitute | No viable substitute, very high switching cost |
| **Bargaining Power of Buyers** | How much power do customers have? | Few large buyers, commodity product, easy to switch | Many fragmented buyers, differentiated product, high switching cost |
| **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** | How much power do input providers have? | Few suppliers, unique inputs, high switching cost to change supplier | Many suppliers, commoditized inputs, easy to switch |

### Using the Framework

1. Evaluate each force as High / Medium / Low for your industry.
2. Identify which forces are changing (increasing threat or decreasing).
3. Develop strategies specifically targeting the highest-threat forces.
4. Reassess annually or when market conditions change significantly.

### Porter Pitfalls

- **Treating it as static.** Five Forces is a snapshot. The real value is in tracking how each force is changing over time.
- **Defining the industry too broadly or too narrowly.** "The software industry" is too broad. "The project management SaaS market for mid-market companies" is about right.
- **Ignoring complements.** Porter's model doesn't account for complementary products that increase value. In tech, complements (e.g., apps on a platform) often matter more than substitutes.

## Blue Ocean Strategy

Blue Ocean Strategy argues that lasting competitive advantage comes from creating uncontested market space (blue oceans) rather than fighting over existing markets (red oceans).

### Red vs Blue Oceans

| Dimension | Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Market | Existing, well-defined | Unknown, to be created |
| Competition | Beat the competition | Make competition irrelevant |
| Strategy | Differentiate or low cost | Both differentiate AND low cost |
| Focus | Capture existing demand | Create and capture new demand |
| Approach | Trade-off value vs cost | Break the value-cost trade-off |

### The Strategy Canvas

A strategy canvas is a visual tool for comparing your offering with competitors along the key factors of competition.

**To build one:**

1. Identify the 5-10 factors that the industry competes on (price, features, service, etc.)
2. Plot competitors' positions on each factor (high = more investment/offering)
3. Plot your current position
4. Design your future position by applying the Four Actions Framework

### The Four Actions Framework

| Action | Question | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors the industry takes for granted should be eliminated? | Southwest eliminated meals, seat assignments, first class |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should be reduced well below industry standard? | Southwest reduced in-flight service, hub-and-spoke connectivity |
| **Raise** | Which factors should be raised well above industry standard? | Southwest raised frequency, on-time performance, employee culture |
| **Create** | Which factors that the industry has never offered should be created? | Cirque du Soleil created theatrical production values in circus |

### Blue Ocean Pitfalls

- **Confusing "new to us" with "new to the world."** A blue ocean is about creating a new value curve, not just entering a new market.
- **Forgetting execution.** A brilliant blue ocean strategy fails without aligned processes, incentives, and culture.
- **Believing blue oceans are permanent.** Eventually, imitators arrive. The strategy should include defense and renewal plans.

## Competitive Moat Frameworks

A moat is a structural advantage that allows a company to sustain above-market returns over time. Not all advantages are moats — a moat must be durable and defensible.

### Moat Types

| Moat Type | Description | Durability | Examples |
|-----------|-------------|------------|----------|
| **Network Effects** | Value increases as more users join | Very high (winner-take-most) | Meta, Uber, Airbnb |
| **Switching Costs** | Cost to leave prevents churn | High if persistent | Salesforce, SAP, AWS |
| **Economies of Scale** | Unit costs decrease with volume | Moderate (requires scale) | Amazon, Costco |
| **Intangible Assets** | Patents, brands, regulatory licenses | Variable (patents expire) | Pharma brands, Coca-Cola |
| **Cost Advantages** | Structural cost advantages (location, process, access) | Moderate (competitors can replicate) | Southwest, Walmart |
| **Data/Algorithm Moats** | Unique data enables better products | Diminishing (data as commodity) | Google Search, Waze |

### Moat Assessment

For each advantage, assess:

1. **Durability.** How many years will this advantage persist without reinforcement?
2. **Defensibility.** If a well-funded competitor enters, can they replicate this advantage?
3. **Scalability.** Does the moat strengthen or weaken as the company grows?
4. **Relevance.** Does this moat matter in the current market? (A 1990s distribution moat is worthless in an online world.)
5. **Renewability.** Can the company invest to strengthen the moat, or is it on a timer?

### Moat Pitfalls

- **First-mover advantage is not a moat.** Being first gives you a head start, not a permanent advantage. Many first movers are overtaken.
- **Brand is a weak moat without reinforcement.** Brand loyalty degrades without continued investment in quality and experience.
- **Patents are a time-limited moat.** If patents are the primary moat, the company has a ticking clock.
- **Confusing scale with economies of scale.** Big is not the same as structurally cheaper. A big company with high cost structure has no advantage.

## Competitive Positioning

Positioning is about occupying a distinct place in the customer's mind. It's not about the product — it's about the perception of the product relative to alternatives.

### The Positioning Statement Template

```
For [target customer]
Who [customer need]
Our product is a [category]
That [key benefit].
Unlike [competitor alternative],
We [unique differentiator].
```

**Example:**
> For mid-market engineering leaders who need to ship software faster and more reliably, our product is a CI/CD platform that provides a fully managed, zero-ops pipeline experience. Unlike self-hosted Jenkins or GitLab CI, we eliminate infrastructure management and provide built-in security scanning and deployment automation.

### Positioning Maps

A positioning map plots competitors on two dimensions that customers care about. The goal is to find an underserved quadrant.

| Dimension Options | Examples |
|-------------------|----------|
| Price vs Quality | Low price + low quality → High price + high quality |
| Breadth vs Depth | Broad features + shallow → Narrow focus + deep |
| Scale vs Service | Enterprise scale + low service → Boutique + high service |
| Speed vs Customization | Fast + standardized → Slow + bespoke |

### Positioning Pitfalls

- **Trying to be everything to everyone.** The most successful companies are the most narrowly focused in their positioning. They own a specific space.
- **Feature-based differentiation.** Customers don't buy features. They buy outcomes. "We have 50 integrations" is a feature. "Your team can connect any tool in 5 minutes" is an outcome.
- **Ignoring the competitive frame.** You don't choose your positioning in a vacuum. Your customers already categorize you. Understand their current frame before trying to change it.
